**What is Credit Risk?**  
- Credit risk is defined as **the possibility that a borrower will default** on repaying the loan.  
- This risk affects the lender (bank), which may suffer losses if the borrower fails to repay.  
- The risk is not limited to the principal amount lent but extends to the **total amount repayable**, including interest.  
- Example: Given a loan of ₹10 lakh, with interest the repayment amount might be ₹12.5 lakh. The credit risk covers the full ₹12.5 lakh, not just the ₹10 lakh principal.  

This broadens the understanding of credit risk as encompassing total exposure (principal + interest).

[00:02:48]  
**Credit Worthiness and the 5 Cs of Credit Risk**  
- Before granting a loan, banks evaluate the borrower's **creditworthiness** using the well-established framework of the **5 Cs of credit risk**. These are:  

| 5 Cs of Credit Risk | Definition                                                                                  |
|---------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Character           | The borrower's reputation and track record for repaying debts                              |
| Capacity            | The borrower's ability to repay the loan based on income and financial stability           |
| Collateral          | Assets pledged by the borrower (like a house in home loans) that the bank can seize if default occurs  |
| Conditions          | Economic and market conditions, including regional GDP, income levels, and economic outlook |
| Capital             | The borrower's own net worth or equity, which indicates financial strength                   |

- Example of **collateral**: In a home loan, the house itself acts as collateral, which the bank can sell to recover money if repayments fail.

This section conveys how banks systematically assess borrower risk profiles.

[00:05:48]  
**Introduction to Credit Risk Management and Example**  
- Credit risk management involves methods and processes used by banks to measure, control, and mitigate the risk that borrowers will not repay loans.  
- The speaker introduces the highly relevant **2008 U.S. financial crisis** as a real-world example of credit risk failure:  
  - Excessive issuance of house loans without adequate assessment of borrowers' creditworthiness.  
  - Resulted in widespread defaults and forced sales of homes.  
  - Oversupply of houses coupled with diminished demand caused market imbalance.  
  - The failure of major banks like **Lehman Brothers** (one of the biggest financial firms) highlighted the consequences of poor credit risk management.  

This example underscores the systemic risks posed by credit defaults and the critical need for regulatory frameworks.

[00:07:48]  
**Regulatory Response and Default Definition**  
- **Default** is defined as the inability of the borrower to meet their financial obligations and a declaration that repayment is not possible.  
- Post-crisis, regulatory bodies implemented **stringent guidelines** to prevent loan defaults and protect the financial system.  
- Example of a regulatory body: In India, the **Reserve Bank of India (RBI)** governs banking operations and enforces regulations to ensure credit risk is managed effectively.  

This segment explains the role of regulations and regulatory agencies in enforcing credit risk controls.

**Further details on the IRB approach:**  
- The IRB approach itself is subdivided into two types:

| IRB Type      | Description                                                   |
|---------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Foundation IRB (F-IRB)** | Banks estimate Probability of Default (PD); other parameters are set by regulators.|
| **Advanced IRB (A-IRB)**   | Banks estimate PD, Loss Given Default (LGD), and Exposure At Default (EAD) internally.|

**Note:** The **Advanced IRB (A-IRB)** is the more sophisticated method and therefore more widely applied in banks.

The presenter highlights the **key metric for credit risk management** being calculated across these models:  

- **Expected Credit Loss (ECL)**, which is the projected average loss from credit defaults.

[00:07:28]  
The fundamental formula for estimating credit loss is expressed as:  

$$
\text{ECL} = \text{PD} \times \text{LGD} \times \text{EAD}
$$

Where:  
- $PD$ = **Probability of Default**: The likelihood that a borrower will default.  
- $LGD$ = **Loss Given Default**: The proportion of exposure lost if default occurs.  
- $EAD$ = **Exposure at Default**: The total value exposed to loss at the time of default.

### Key Insights

- **Basel and IFRS frameworks** are dominant regulatory guidelines for credit risk management across Europe and the US respectively.  
- **Basel's three pillars** establish a comprehensive approach:  
  - Capital adequacy (Pillar 1),  
  - Supervisory oversight (Pillar 2),  
  - Market transparency and discipline (Pillar 3).  
- **Minimum Capital Requirement** is crucial for banks to absorb losses without disrupting financial activities.  
- Two capital adequacy calculation approaches exist:  
  - **Standardized Approach (SA)** relies on external ratings and rules, easier but less risk-sensitive.  
  - **Internal Ratings-Based (IRB) Approach** allows banks to use internal estimates of credit parameters, enhancing accuracy.  
- IRB further splits into **Foundation IRB** and **Advanced IRB**, with the latter widely favored for its sophistication.  
- The core calculation for **Expected Credit Loss (ECL)** centers on three parameters: $PD$, $LGD$, and $EAD$. This metric anticipates likely losses from credit defaults.

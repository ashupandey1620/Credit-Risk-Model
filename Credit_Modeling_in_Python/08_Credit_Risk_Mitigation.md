### What is Credit Risk? Basic Definitions and Context  
- **Credit risk** is defined from a banking perspective, targeting freshers who may be unfamiliar with the term.  
- **Credit**: Funds lent to a borrower, who can be:  
  - A **retail borrower** (individual/household).  
  - A **commercial borrower** (firm, company, enterprise).  
- **Risk**: Any event causing the bank to experience loss—financial, reputational, market-related, operational, liquidity risks, etc.  
- **Credit risk**, therefore, is the risk that the **loan or credit extended will not be fully repaid**, causing loss to the bank.  

[00:03:07]  
### Historical Context and Importance of Credit Risk  
- Refers to the **2008 financial crisis** triggered by the collapse of Lehman Brothers, which caused massive losses across the banking sector.  
- Post-crisis, regulators (such as the **Federal Reserve in the US, OSFI in Canada, and RBI in India**) mandated stricter capital requirements and credit risk management to safeguard banking stability.  
- Banks are required to maintain **capital buffers** to absorb potential losses and protect depositor funds and overall economic stability.  

[00:04:46]  
### Role of Banks and Fund Flow in the Economy  
- Banks act as **intermediaries**, channeling funds from **suppliers** (depositors/savers) to **demanders** (borrowers).  
- Demand for funds arises from individuals and businesses needing loans to buy homes, expand business, etc.  
- Supply of funds is mostly from depositors whose money banks lend out.  
- Banks earn profit through the **interest rate spread** between lending and deposit rates.

### Risk Segmentation in Lending  
- Lending to **low-risk segments** like individual homeowners offers collateral security (e.g., house mortgage).  
- Lending to **high-risk segments** such as large enterprises involves more uncertainty due to market fluctuations impacting their ability to repay.  
- Understanding these dynamics is crucial for credit risk assessment.

### Step 1: The 5Cs of Credit Risk  
- **Character:** Evaluates borrower’s credit history, credit score, and payment behavior.  
- **Capacity:** Borrower’s ability to repay the loan, including income and financial stability.  
- **Collateral:** Assets pledged (e.g., house mortgage) as security; ownership and pledge rights explained.  
- **Conditions:** Macro and microeconomic conditions impacting borrower and credit environment (e.g., war, COVID-19 pandemic).  
- **Capital:** The borrower’s investment or down payment as indication of their commitment and financial strength.  
- The 5Cs form a **framework for banks to identify and mitigate inherent risks in lending**.

[00:11:24]  
### Step 2: Robust Credit Policies and Procedures  
- Emphasizes preparing **well-defined credit policies** to guide decision-making and protect against financial risk.  
- Cover terms of sale, credit limits, and collection procedures to effectively manage lending risk.  
- Establishes the foundation for **effective credit risk management**.  

[00:12:30]  
### Step 3: Robust Underwriting Standards  
- Underwriting involves detailed review and approval of loan applications by specialized personnel (“underwriters”).  
- Strong underwriting policies ensure only **eligible borrowers with manageable risk profiles are approved**.  
- Credit history, income assessment, repayment capacity, and existing obligations are evaluated.  
- Underwriters help to minimize adverse selection, safeguard profitability, and set loan terms including interest rates.  
- Underwriting jobs are prevalent in major banks worldwide, including City Bank, Wells Fargo, HSBC, etc.

[00:14:45]  
### Step 4: Adequate Allowance for Loan and Lease Losses (ALLL)  
- ALLL acts as a **reserve for bad debts**, where loans partly or fully unpaid are accounted for in bank losses.  
- The bank must maintain **capital reserves sufficient to absorb estimated credit losses** and ensure financial stability.  
- **Regulatory guidance** (e.g., FDIC) mandates program development to regularly review reserve adequacy.  
- Banks must **clearly understand the purpose of these reserves** and must not use them for generating profits.

[00:17:14]  
### Step 5: Credit Risk Monitoring Process  
- Involves **regular, periodic monitoring of borrower risk profiles** to track changes and emerging risks.  
- Activities include:  
  - Assessing credit score changes.  
  - Monitoring bankruptcy filings, business structure changes, lawsuits, management changes.  
  - Timely follow-ups on overdue payments and account reviews.  
- Uses **model validation and monitoring teams** to continually assess borrower portfolios, supporting risk mitigation.  
- This function offers numerous employment opportunities in banks and financial institutions worldwide.

[00:19:29]  
### Step 6: Stress Testing  
- Stress tests evaluate a bank's **ability to withstand adverse economic conditions** such as recessions or pandemics.  
- Helps identify **vulnerabilities or weak points in loan and lease portfolios**, particularly in sensitive regions or sectors.  
- Ensures banks maintain **capital adequacy ratios (CAR)** to comply with regulatory standards and sustain business during crises.  
- Communication of risk findings to stakeholders ensures informed decision-making at all levels.

### Step 7: Scenario-Based Analysis

- Banks develop **possible future economic scenarios** to foresee potential credit losses.  
- Identifies areas requiring increased attention and informs **proactive risk mitigation strategies**.

### Historical Case: The Lehman Brothers Collapse and Its Lessons  
- **Lehman Brothers bankruptcy (2008)** was triggered by excessive lending to risky borrowers who failed to repay.  
- Resulted in massive loan defaults, a glut of housing supply, collapsing property values, and triggered a systemic crisis.  
- Other banks and investment firms were similarly affected; some were merged or purchased (e.g., Merrill Lynch by Bank of America; Bear Stearns by JP Morgan; Green Whelan by Standard Chartered).  
- The crisis underlined the **critical importance of credit risk management** and became the foundation for modern credit risk frameworks globally.
